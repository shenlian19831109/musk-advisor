# Part 2: Case Study Deep Dives

## Case 1: Model 3 Production Crisis (2017-2018)

### Background

- Model 3 unveiled 2016: 500K+ reservations
- Promised: 5K/week by end 2017
- Actual end 2017: ~1K/week
- Musk slept at factory

### First Principles Analysis

```
Surface Problem: Can't ramp up production
    ↓
Why?
    ├── Battery pack line bottleneck
    ├── Over-automation (robots can't handle flexibility)
    └── Supplier issues

Musk's First Question:
"Why can't we build it ourselves?"
    ↓
Because automation is expensive?
→ Cost matters, but speed matters more
→ Waiting for suppliers = Death
    ↓
Solutions:
├── Build tent for temp production line
├── Add humans, slow automation temporarily
├── Be engineer, not just CEO
└── Sleep at factory, solve problems in real-time
```

### What Musk Learned

```
Automation isn't always the answer:
→ Good for: Standardized, high-volume, low-variation
→ Bad for: Flexible manufacturing, fast iteration

CEO must understand technology:
→ Only with tech understanding can you judge who's telling truth
→ Only on the ground can you make fast decisions
```

### Transferable Thinking

1. **Identify bottlenecks**: Solve the most expensive constraint first
2. **Speed > Perfection**: Fast iteration beats perfect planning
3. **Lead on the ground**: Can't solve production problems from office

## Case 2: SpaceX Falcon 1 - Three Failures (2006-2008)

### Background

- 2006: First launch, rocket crashed shortly after liftoff
- 2007: Second launch, rocket reached altitude but broke apart
- 2008: Third launch, rocket succeeded but satellite didn't reach orbit

### First Principles Analysis

```
Problem: Rocket launches fail
    ↓
Why do traditional rockets fail?
    ├── Launch costs too high (no incentive to innovate)
    └── NASA bureaucracy

Musk's First Question:
"Why must rockets be disposable?"
    ↓
Break it down:
→ What percentage of rocket cost is materials? → ~2%
→ What is the other 98%? → R&D/amortized failures/one-time design
    ↓
Conclusion:
→ If rockets could be recovered and reused, cost could drop 90%+
→ But all experts said impossible
    ↓
Musk's Judgment:
"Physically possible, just difficult"
```

### After Third Failure (48 Hours)

```
Sep 2008: Falcon 1 third launch
Result: Rocket succeeded but satellite didn't reach orbit

48 hours later:
Musk gathers team → "We'll succeed on fourth try"
    │
    ├── Analyze third failure cause
    ├── Fourth launch ready in 3 days (normally 6 weeks)
    └── Sep 28, 2008: Falcon 1 SUCCESS!

Same time: SpaceX had last chance for funding
    → Falcon 1 success → NASA contract → Survival
```

### Transferable Thinking

1. **Failure is process, not result**
2. **Fastest failure iteration = Path to success**
3. **Persistence during hardest times = Necessary condition for survival**

## Case 3: Twitter - 80% Layoffs (2022)

### Background

- Oct 2022: Musk buys Twitter for $44B
- Then: 7,500 employees
- 3 months later: ~1,300 (80%+ laid off)

### First Principles Analysis

```
Problem: Twitter unprofitable
    ↓
Why losing money?
    ├── Too many employees → High labor costs
    ├── High server costs → Bloated architecture
    ├── Single ad revenue → Over-dependent on big clients
    └── Low efficiency → Bureaucracy

First Question:
"How many people does Twitter really need?"
    ↓
His methods:
├── Cancel remote work → Prove many weren't really working
├── Explain WHY in meetings → Leave if don't understand
├── Meet engineers directly → No layered reporting
├── 3-month probation → Leave if can't prove value

Result:
→ After 80% layoffs, Twitter (X) continued running
→ 2023: Started making small profit
```

### Controversy & Reflection

```
Was Musk's approach too harsh?

His defense:
"Those who stayed worked 2X harder"
"People who couldn't accept high intensity shouldn't have been there"

Criticism:
"Many laid off people were actually very good"
"Can this culture sustain?"

Transferable Thinking:
1. Know the core problem you're solving
2. Flat structure = Fast info flow = Fast decisions
3. Cost: High employee turnover + High-pressure culture
```

## Case 4: Tesla Shanghai Factory (2020)

### Background

- 2018: Tesla in production hell, cash flow tight
- 2019: Build factory in China, mass produce 2020
- Key turning point: Shanghai factory made Tesla profitable

### First Principles Analysis

```
Musk's First Question:
"Why can Tesla succeed in China?"

Analysis:
├── Policy support: Govt needs Tesla to build EV supply chain
├── Supply chain: China has world's most complete EV supply chain
├── Cost: Labor/land/logistics far lower than US
└── Market: World's largest EV market

Why Tesla, not other EV makers?
→ Tesla has brand + technology + Musk's mission
→ Other domestic EVs only compete at low end

Result:
→ Shanghai factory: 50%+ of Tesla global production
→ Q3 2020: First full year profit
```

### Transferable Thinking

1. **Use local advantages**, don't compete, collaborate
2. **Mission + Business = Stronger competitiveness**
3. **Fastest growth = Go to right place at right time**

# Part 6: Practice Exercises

## Exercise 1: First Principles Analysis

**Topic: "Why is the food delivery industry unprofitable"**

```
Background:
- Meituan/Ele.me dominate most markets
- But delivery platforms generally unprofitable or razor-thin margins
- Rider costs/subsidies/delivery/infrastructure high

Step 1: List Common Assumptions

Assumption 1: "Rider costs too high, so unprofitable"
Assumption 2: "Platform subsidy wars, so losing money"
Assumption 3: "Users price-sensitive, won't pay delivery fees"

Step 2: Question Each Assumption

Assumption 1: Are rider costs really high?
→ Average rider monthly income: $800-1100
→ Per-delivery fee: $1-1.5
→ Per-order value: $5-8
→ Rider cost %: 15-25%
→ Actually: Already lower than restaurant labor costs

Assumption 2: Are subsidies the cause?
→ Subsidies were to grab market share
→ Could market share be maintained without subsidies?
→ Seems not, user loyalty is low

Assumption 3: Do users refuse to pay delivery fees?
→ Actually willing to pay, just need "free delivery" scenario
→ Shows: Price sensitive, but not absolute rejection

Step 3: Break Down to Basic Elements

What IS the core value of food delivery?
→ Users don't need to go out + don't need to cook + fast delivery

What costs does this value require?
→ Traffic costs: acquire users
→ Delivery costs: riders deliver food
→ Merchant costs: attract restaurants
→ Tech costs: platform development/maintenance

Step 4: Reconstruct from Zero

If starting from scratch, no baggage:
→ Don't build an APP, what else?
→ Just build "rider dispatch algorithm" and sell to restaurants?
→ Just build "restaurant POS system" and extend?
→ Just serve "high-end restaurants delivery"?

Thinking: Why must current players do full chain?
→ Without full chain, user experience can't close loop
→ But full chain = High costs

Conclusion: Food delivery's "Impossible Triangle":
→ Low price + Fast delivery + High quality service
→ Can't satisfy all three simultaneously
```

## Exercise 2: 10X Thinking

**Topic: "How to reduce customer churn rate from 20% to 2%"**

```
Current: Customer churn rate 20%

Conventional 10% improvement plans:
→ Improve customer service quality
→ Increase customer points system
→ Lower prices
→ Add features

Ask yourself: These are optimizations, not reconstructions

10X Thinking version:

Problem reframe: What would make churn rate 20% → 2%?

Not "how to make leaving customers satisfied", but "how to make customers unable to leave"

Solution exploration:

Solution A: Bind customer's data/content
→ Customer data created on my platform can't be migrated
→ Example: All project files for design software on my cloud

Solution B: Become core of customer's workflow
→ Not "using my product", but "my product IS the work itself"
→ Example: Slack as team's core communication, replacement cost high

Solution C: Build community belonging
→ Customers not just buying product, joining community
→ Example: Apple users vs Android users

Solution D: Price reconstruction
→ Not "lower price", but "don't let customer feel like spending"
→ Example: Product becomes low monthly fee but feels "premium"

Selection criteria:
→ Which is physically possible?
→ Which cost structure can support?
→ Which are competitors NOT doing?
```
